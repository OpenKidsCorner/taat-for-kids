#### Observations

- The complex diagrams and the length of the worksheet triggered the familiar initial reaction — hesitation, some resistance, a few saying they could not do it. But with a little encouragement, everyone got started and made genuine attempts. This pattern has appeared in almost every session; the barrier is psychological more than intellectual.

- Binary addition was a sticking point for some students. They had not fully studied or practiced it since it was first introduced, and the gaps showed. Explanations had to be given on the spot before those students could proceed.

- Given the number of problems, many students only managed to complete the first three or four questions within the session time. The worksheet was not designed to be finished in one sitting, and that is fine — what mattered was the attempt and the thinking along the way.

### Building with AI — Natural Language in Action

With the worksheet time done, everyone was asked to open their laptops and build something using AI — continuing from where session 4 had left off. Predictably, the kids gravitated straight toward games and started playing as soon as something was running on screen.

For a couple of students, the first prompt did not produce a working game. Rather than giving up, they refined their prompts and kept trying until something worked — which is exactly the right instinct. One student's second version broke something that the first had working, so he reverted to the earlier version and played from there.

When building games was gently challenged — could they make something other than a game, like a quiz? — one group found their own answer: a car racing game that paused at intervals to ask a quiz question. A wrong answer cost a life. And because they wanted to play as long as possible, they set themselves more than seven lives from the start. Alhamdulillah — they had understood the system well enough to game it.

#### Observations

- The shift from building to playing happened almost immediately once a game was running. The line between "building an application" and "playing it" is thin at this age, and perhaps that is not entirely a problem — the motivation to play is what kept them iterating when things did not work.

- Prompt iteration was a natural behaviour for some students. When the first output failed, they did not stop — they tried again with different wording. This is a real skill at the natural language layer of the tower, and it emerged without being taught explicitly.

- The quiz-plus-racing game was the standout moment. It showed genuine creative thinking — combining two ideas and then immediately reasoning about the rules to their own advantage. The teacher's nudge away from pure games produced something more interesting than either a game or a quiz alone.

- Free-tier prompt limits cut the session short for some students. Those without a paid account ran out before they could build anything substantial. This is a practical constraint worth planning around in future sessions — either by identifying free alternatives or by pairing free-tier students with those who have more access.

## Key Takeaways

**For the students:**
- The first reaction to a complex diagram or a long worksheet is not the true one — hesitation and resistance are normal, but they pass. Every student who started, made progress. Starting is the skill.
- Binary addition is not something you learn once and keep — it needs regular practice. Understanding why the carry-forward works is more durable than knowing that `1 + 1 = 10` as a rule.
- When a prompt does not produce what you wanted, the answer is to refine and try again — not to give up. That instinct is real and valuable at the natural language layer of the tower.
- Building something interesting often means combining ideas. A game and a quiz separately are ordinary; a racing game that quizzes you mid-race and rewards longer play is something the students invented themselves.

**For the teacher:**
- Notes must be recorded on the same day. What is not written while it is fresh is largely gone within a week. These two sessions are proof of that. A simple habit — even a few bullet points at the end of each session — would have preserved far more.
- The initial fear reaction to complex problems is consistent across sessions. A single moment of verbal encouragement at the start of a hard task is reliably enough to get everyone moving. Build it in deliberately rather than waiting for the resistance to appear.
- Binary addition gaps keep resurfacing on demand rather than being addressed structurally. A short dedicated drill at the start of the next few sessions — five minutes, not more — would close the gap faster than re-explaining it each time a student gets stuck.
- Free-tier prompt limits cut the AI-building activity short for some students. This needs to be planned around before the next similar session — whether through pairing, a shared account, or identifying tools with more generous free limits.
- The nudge away from "just build a game" produced the most creative outcome of both days. Gentle constraints push students toward more interesting thinking. Worth making this a deliberate part of the AI-building activity going forward.
