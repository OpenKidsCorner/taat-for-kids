# Session 1 — Introduction to Technology
**Date:** April 04, 2026 | **Duration:** ~4.25 hours | **Age group:** 8–12

بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم

Alhamdulillah, Allah made this first session possible. We came in with a simple plan, no idea how the kids would respond, and Allah blessed the day beyond what we had imagined. May He accept it, forgive our shortcomings, and put lasting benefit in it for the children.

---

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this session, if Allah willed it for them, students were able to:

- Articulate their own understanding of what a computer is
- Recognize that communicating with a machine requires a specific **language** — a fixed, limited vocabulary
- Write a sequence of instructions (FORWARD, LEFT, RIGHT) to move a "robot" in a square
- Begin to reason about what **new instructions** would be needed to draw shapes beyond a square — and why

---

## How the Day Unfolded

### Physical Warm-up — Football with Golden Goal Rule (~40 min)

Alhamdulillah, we started outside. The kids played football with a golden goal rule — first goal wins the game. It was energetic, a little chaotic, and exactly what they needed.

In between the running, we paused to talk about the game itself: how do you cover a player from the opposite side? How do you run into open space to receive the ball? These were small conversations, but they planted an early seed — that thinking ahead, reading the situation, and moving with intention matters. We did not make the connection to programming explicitly yet. We just let them play.

---

### Wuzu and Nafl Salah — Asking Allah for Help (~20 min)

Before starting the classroom, kids were asked to perform wuzu and nafl salah. One of the elder kids led the prayers. May Allah help us continue this to seek help each time to learn easily, to make the session beneficial, and to forgive us for our mistakes.

### Discussion Session (~2 hours)

Alhamdulillah, this was the heart of the day. What follows is roughly how it went — not perfectly, not in a straight line, but honestly.

---

#### Introductions

Each kid was first asked to write their introduction on paper. Immediately, the room came alive — some asked what to write, some asked for spellings of words, some started discussing ideas with the person next to them, and a few just started writing without asking much.

Then, one by one, they came to the front and shared. Alhamdulillah, everyone confidently shared whatever they had wrote.

---

#### What Do You Know About Technology?

When asked about technology, the room lit up. The responses were exactly what you would expect from kids this age — cartoons, games, buying fun stuff online, cool gadgets. There was real excitement in how they talked about it.

Mixed in, almost as an afterthought, were things like PowerPoint, CapCut, Google Classroom, Zoom, news websites, their parents' business websites. A couple of kids mentioned Python and C++. Alhamdulillah — there was more there than it first appeared.

---

#### What Is a Computer?

When asked to write their understanding of a computer, Some wrote the basic components — mouse, keyboard, screen. Others went further: motherboard, CPU. The mouse, of all things, became a small moment of its own — kids comapred computer mouse with a real mouse, a couple even drew a picture of a mouse instead of writing the word, which made everyone laugh and led to a brief side conversation.

Some kids wrote about what they had actually *done* on a computer: PowerPoint, Zoom for online classes. A couple had already done some coding. Alhamdulillah, everyone had their own answers.

---

#### How Do We Communicate? — Towards "Language"

The next question: *How do we communicate?* Kids immediately said: mouth, tongue, hand signs. The discussion moved naturally, and the word being guided towards was **language** (though none was able to identify they word -itself) — that to communicate, you need a shared language. Urdu, English, sign language. This landed well.

---

#### The AC Remote — A Machine's Language

With "language" now in the room, attention turned to the AC remote control on the wall. How do we talk to the AC?

Kids identified the buttons — temperature up and down, fan speed, mode. Then, as kids tend to do, they went further: you could turn it off from the breaker. Cut the electricity. Kick it hard enough to damage it. In modern times, control it through a mobile app or even by voice.

Then came the more interesting question: *Can you make the AC do something it is not designed to do?* The answer, slowly, was no. You are always limited to what the AC itself is capable of. The remote is just the language — the vocabulary available to you. The machine decides what words exist.

---

#### The Simplest Case — The Light Switch

To make the point even cleaner, attention moved to the simplest device in the room: the electric bulb. One button. Two options. On or off. That is the entire language.

---

#### The Robot Activity — LEFT, RIGHT, FORWARD

This is where things got wonderfully messy, and alhamdulillah for it.

The instructor announced: *"I am a robot. I only understand three words: LEFT, RIGHT, and FORWARD."* The task was to make the robot walk in a square.

Before the question was even finished, instructions started flying. When the robot (Instructor) dutifully followed — the instructions would lead the robot to either outside of the room through the door, or into the washroom next to the room. They woould open the door. But, the robot would stop listing and turn into the real instructor again. The whole activity was fun and noisy with instructions coming from every where.

One kid asked: *"Is there an instruction to turn you on and off? Because if yes, we'll just turn you off and go play."*

After the initial excitement and laughter — alhamdulillah for the energy in the room — everyone settled. Each kid wrote their solution on paper, came up to the front, gave instructions one at a time, and watched the robot move. With a few tries, corrections, and some laughing at wrong turns, everyone found a solution.

The solutions that came up:

```
F-L-F-L-F-L-F
F-R-F-R-F-R-F
F-F-L-F-F-L-F-F-L-F-F
```

Then came a small challenge: *"Come up with a solution that starts differently — and there's a prize."* After some thinking, one kid came up with:

```
R-F-R-F-R-F-R-F
```

Starting with a turn before the first step. Alhamdulillah — that kind of thinking is exactly what we were hoping to see.

---

### Cricket Break (~30 min)

We went outside again. A 3-over match. Everyone came back tired, which was fine — the second half of the session was quieter and more individual anyway. Alhamdulillah for the energy kids bring.

---

### Beyond the Square — Which Shapes Can the Robot Draw?

Back in the classroom, different quadrilateral shapes were shown: parallelogram, rhombus, rectangle, square, kite, trapezoid.

The question: *Which of these can the robot draw with only LEFT, RIGHT, and FORWARD?*

Initially, most said all of them. So the follow-up came: *"Okay — make me draw a parallelogram."*

The robot only turns 90 degrees. A parallelogram needs angles that are not 90 degrees. This is when some of the kids understood the limitation. Not all at once — this was the point where everyone started moving at their own pace.

---

### Individual Pace — Working Through the Shapes

From here, the session became more individual. Kids would bring their written answers, and the discussion would happen one-on-one or in small clusters. Some had already overheard a nearby conversation and had adjusted their thinking. Others worked through it slowly and steadily. A few had discussed it among themselves before coming up.

Alhamdulillah, eventually everyone was able to categorize the shapes — which ones the robot could draw, which ones it could not.

---

### What New Instructions Would We Need?

The final discussion, done individually as kids reached it: *If we wanted the robot to draw the shapes it currently cannot — what new instructions would we need to add?*

Answers varied:
- Some said: **Diagonal**
- Some said: **Zig-zag**
- One said: **Specify the degree** — turn by a specific angle, not just 90 degrees

Then the test: *Give me the instruction.* When a kid said "Diagonal" and the robot began turning in only one direction, they would catch it. *Diagonal-right. Diagonal-left.* Or: *Turn 45 degrees right.* The precision of the instruction started to matter.

A couple of kids worked through instructions for two or three different shapes. Some completed one. Some did not quite finish. And that was okay.

---

### Wrapping Up

The session did not end with a clean finish. It wound down the way honest sessions do — energy fading, a few kids still working through their last shape, everyone deciding together that they would complete the instructions for all quadrilaterals at home.

---

## Key Ideas from This Session

- Every device has a **language** — a fixed, pre-decided set of instructions it understands. You cannot go beyond what the machine is built to do.
- The **order** of instructions matters. The same words in a different order give a different result.
- There is often **more than one correct solution** — some are shorter than others.
- When a machine cannot do something, the answer is to **extend the language** — define new instructions. But someone has to decide precisely what those instructions mean.

## Practice Activities

These are a couple of activities the kids can do at home, if Allah wills. May Allah make it easy for them.

**Instructions key:**
- **F** — move one step Forward (in whichever direction you are currently facing)
- **R** — turn 90° to the Right (you do not move)
- **L** — turn 90° to the Left (you do not move)
- **DR** — turn 45° to the Diagonal-Right (you do not move)
- **DL** — turn 45° to the Diagonal-Left (you do not move)

---

### Activity 1 — Squares and Rectangles

*Use only F, R, and L.*

The first row is done as an example. Try to fill in at least two different instruction sets for each shape — sets that trace the same diagram in different ways.

| **Square — 1 step per side** *(example)* | F R F R F R F | F L F L F L F |

| Rectangle — 2 steps × 3 steps | | |
| Rectangle — 1 step × 4 steps | | |
| Rectangle of your own size | | |

### Activity 2 — Quadrilaterals and More

*Use F, R, L, DR, and DL.*

The first row is done as an example. For each shape, think carefully about how many degrees you need to turn at each corner — and which combination of R, L, DR, and DL gives you that turn.

| Diagram | Instruction Set 1 | Instruction Set 2 |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Regular Octagon — 1 step per side** *(example)* | F DR F DR F DR F DR F DR F DR F DR F DR | F DL F DL F DL F DL F DL F DL F DL F DL |
| Rhombus (diamond — all 4 sides diagonal) | | |
| Parallelogram (2 horizontal sides + 2 diagonal sides) | | |
| Trapezoid | | |
| Kite | | |
| Irregular Pentagon | | |
| Irregular Hexagon | | |
| A shape of your own choice | | |

> **Hint for Activity 2:** At each corner, ask yourself — how many degrees does the robot need to turn? A right angle is 90° (one R or L). A diagonal angle is 45° (one DR or DL). Two DRs in a row = 90°, same as one R. Three DRs = 135°. Can you figure out which shapes are impossible with only these instructions — and why?
